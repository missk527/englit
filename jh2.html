Additional Predicted AQA Essay Questions

The Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde by Robert Louis Stevenson
Five more prediction questions with extracts and extended Grade 9 model answers

Question 1: How does Stevenson present Mr Utterson as an important character?

1 Mr Utterson the lawyer was a man of a rugged countenance, that was never lighted by a smile; 2 cold, scanty and embarrassed in discourse; backward in sentiment; lean, long, dusty, dreary, and yet somehow lovable. 3 At friendly meetings, and when the wine was to his taste, something eminently human beaconed from his eye; 4 something indeed which never found its way into his talk, but which spoke not only in these silent symbols of the after-dinner face, 5 but more often and loudly in the acts of his life.

Starting with this extract, write about how Stevenson presents Mr Utterson as an important character in The Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde.

Grade 9 Model Answer

Stevenson presents Utterson as an important character because he acts as the moral centre, the rational investigator and the reader’s guide through the mystery of the novel. In this extract, his description immediately establishes him as austere and emotionally restrained. The list “cold, scanty and embarrassed in discourse” creates an impression of control and social reserve, while the accumulation of adjectives gives him a dry, almost mechanical quality. However, Stevenson complicates this by ending with the surprisingly affectionate phrase “and yet somehow lovable”. The conjunction “yet” is crucial because it introduces contradiction, suggesting that beneath Utterson’s rigid exterior there is genuine warmth. This dual presentation makes him significant because he embodies the respectable Victorian gentleman while also possessing enough humanity to inspire trust.

Stevenson’s language here is carefully balanced. The harsh monosyllables of “cold” and “scanty” make Utterson appear emotionally inaccessible, but the metaphor “something eminently human beaconed from his eye” introduces a softer, more compassionate side. The verb “beaconed” has connotations of light, guidance and rescue, implying that Utterson functions almost like a moral lighthouse in a world darkened by secrecy and corruption. This is especially important in a Gothic novella filled with locked doors, fog and concealed identities: Utterson represents steadiness and reason against confusion and terror.

Structurally, Stevenson places this description at the beginning of the novel so that Utterson becomes the lens through which the mystery is filtered. The reader learns about Hyde, Jekyll and the central enigma largely through his perspective. This narrative choice is important because it delays revelation and creates suspense. Unlike a more emotional narrator, Utterson’s logical temperament means he gathers evidence slowly and cautiously, which mirrors the methodical pace of the investigation. His role is almost detective-like, particularly later when he declares, “If he be Mr Hyde, I shall be Mr Seek.” The pun gives a moment of lightness, but it also confirms his structural importance as the seeker of truth.

Utterson is also important because Stevenson uses him to explore Victorian values. He is discreet, loyal and deeply concerned with reputation. His tendency to avoid gossip and suppress strong feeling reflects the culture of respectability in which outward self-command is prized. Yet Stevenson subtly critiques this restraint: Utterson’s caution slows the discovery of the truth, suggesting that excessive politeness can allow evil to flourish unchecked. In this way, Utterson is not simply admirable; he also embodies the limitations of the society he belongs to.

Furthermore, Utterson’s importance lies in the contrast he creates with Hyde and Jekyll. Hyde is all impulse, violence and appetite, whereas Utterson is discipline and reason. Jekyll is divided, unstable and self-deceiving, whereas Utterson is comparatively coherent and dependable. Through these contrasts, Stevenson uses Utterson to highlight the dangers of moral fragmentation. Even when he is not the most dramatic character, he remains the stabilising force of the narrative.

Ultimately, Stevenson presents Utterson as important because he is far more than a bystander. He is the moral observer, the structural investigator and the embodiment of respectable society. Through his measured voice and persistent search for truth, Stevenson guides the reader into the darkness of the novella while ensuring there is still a figure of order against the chaos.

Question 2: How does Stevenson present setting and atmosphere in the novel?

1 It was a wild, cold, seasonable night of March, with a pale moon, lying on her back as though the wind had tilted her, 2 and flying wrack of the most diaphanous and lawny texture. 3 The wind made talking difficult, flecked the blood into the face, and worried the scanty flame of the lamp, 4 and rummaged the few light bushes in the garden avenue.

Starting with this extract, write about how Stevenson presents setting and atmosphere in The Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde.

Grade 9 Model Answer

Stevenson presents setting and atmosphere as unstable, threatening and psychologically revealing. In this extract, the weather is immediately personified as aggressive and unnatural. The moon is described as “lying on her back as though the wind had tilted her”, which makes the natural world seem physically disturbed. This violent image suggests a universe that has been knocked off balance, mirroring the moral and psychological disorder at the centre of the novella. Rather than offering a neutral backdrop, the setting becomes an active participant in the narrative’s unease.

The atmosphere is intensified through a semantic field of disturbance. Verbs such as “tilted”, “worried” and “rummaged” imply intrusion and restlessness. Particularly effective is the verb “worried”, which usually describes repeated anxious attack, as though the wind is tormenting the “scanty flame of the lamp”. This image is symbolic: the weak lamp-light suggests fragility, civilisation and knowledge, while the violent wind symbolises chaos and obscurity. Stevenson often uses this tension between light and darkness to dramatise the conflict between order and hidden evil.

His Gothic method is clear here. The setting is not simply dark; it is dynamic and hostile. The phrase “flying wrack” conveys rapid movement and confusion, while the unusual adjective “diaphanous” gives the atmosphere an eerie delicacy. This combination of violence and fragility creates a dreamlike instability. Stevenson’s diction therefore produces a setting that is both beautiful and deeply unsettling, reflecting the novel’s fascination with duality.

Across the novella, London itself is repeatedly presented as divided. There are respectable streets that “shone out in contrast to its dingy neighbourhood”, but also sinister alleys, neglected doorways and fog-filled spaces where moral certainty breaks down. This divided urban landscape mirrors Jekyll’s own split identity. Stevenson uses setting symbolically: the city becomes an external map of the divided self. The respectable front conceals corruption behind it, just as Jekyll’s public image conceals Hyde.

Structurally, setting is used to build suspense and delay understanding. Fog obscures vision, darkness restricts knowledge and enclosed interiors intensify claustrophobia. These devices are not decorative; they influence how the plot unfolds. Characters misread one another, conceal truths and move through spaces that seem designed to hide evidence. The atmosphere therefore supports the mystery plot while also deepening the themes of secrecy and repression.

In a Victorian context, the urban setting may reflect anxieties about modern city life, where beneath apparent order there existed crime, poverty and vice. Stevenson taps into fears that the city, like the human mind, contained hidden regions that respectable society preferred not to acknowledge. The Gothic atmosphere turns these fears into something immediate and sensory.

Ultimately, Stevenson presents setting and atmosphere as central to meaning in the novella. Through personification, symbolism and Gothic imagery, he creates a world in which the environment reflects moral disorder. The threatening atmosphere does not merely surround the characters; it reveals the darkness they are trying to hide.

Question 3: How does Stevenson present Dr Lanyon?

1 He began to go wrong, wrong in mind; and though of course I continue to take an interest in him for old sake's sake, 2 as they say, I see and I have seen devilish little of the man. 3 Such unscientific balderdash would have estranged Damon and Pythias.

Starting with this extract, write about how Stevenson presents Dr Lanyon in The Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde.

Grade 9 Model Answer

Stevenson presents Dr Lanyon as a rational, conventional and ultimately tragic character whose reactions help expose the dangerous extremity of Jekyll’s experiments. In this extract, Lanyon’s repetition of “wrong, wrong in mind” is emphatic and judgemental. The repeated adjective “wrong” suggests both intellectual error and moral deviation, showing that Lanyon sees Jekyll’s scientific behaviour as a form of corruption. The simple repetition also conveys emotional intensity, hinting that his confidence has been shaken more than he wants to admit.

Lanyon’s voice is strikingly different from Jekyll’s speculative and abstract language. Where Jekyll speaks in philosophical terms, Lanyon uses dismissive, forceful phrasing. The insult “unscientific balderdash” is particularly revealing. “Balderdash” trivialises Jekyll’s ideas as nonsense, while the adjective “unscientific” suggests that Lanyon believes proper science must remain within accepted boundaries of reason and evidence. Stevenson therefore uses Lanyon to represent orthodox scientific values. He is not anti-science; rather, he stands for disciplined inquiry against reckless experimentation.

The classical allusion to “Damon and Pythias” is also significant. These figures are associated with ideal friendship and loyalty, so Lanyon’s claim that Jekyll’s behaviour “would have estranged Damon and Pythias” shows how deeply he feels their intellectual disagreement. Stevenson uses the allusion to elevate the conflict beyond a personal quarrel: it becomes a symbolic split between two models of knowledge. Jekyll pursues transgressive discovery, whereas Lanyon remains committed to rational limits.

Structurally, Lanyon is important because he acts as a witness to horror. For much of the novella, he appears solid, sensible and sceptical. This makes his later collapse particularly powerful. When a character so committed to reason is destroyed by what he sees, the reader understands the sheer terror of Hyde’s transformation. Stevenson uses Lanyon’s decline as a narrative strategy to validate the supernatural horror indirectly. If even Lanyon cannot bear the truth, then the truth must be devastating indeed.

Stevenson also presents Lanyon as tragically limited. His certainty and confidence make him initially reassuring, but they also mean that when his worldview is shattered, he has no flexibility with which to survive the shock. In this sense, Lanyon becomes a tragic figure of epistemological collapse: the horror is not only what he sees, but the fact that his framework for understanding reality is destroyed. His death suggests that forbidden knowledge carries unbearable psychological consequences.

In a Victorian context, Lanyon may reflect debates about the purpose and limits of scientific inquiry. The nineteenth century saw extraordinary scientific progress, but also deep anxieties about what might happen when science challenged religion, morality and the natural order. Lanyon voices the respectable response to such fears, whereas Jekyll represents dangerous ambition.

Ultimately, Stevenson presents Lanyon as both a foil to Jekyll and a victim of the truth. Through his rationality, his horror and his collapse, Stevenson shows that the novel’s central experiment is not merely unconventional but fundamentally catastrophic.

Question 4: How does Stevenson present religion and morality?

1 I stood already committed to a profound duplicity of life. 2 Although so profound a double-dealer, I was in no sense a hypocrite; 3 both sides of me were in dead earnest. 4 It was the curse of mankind that these incongruous faggots were thus bound together— 5 that in the agonised womb of consciousness, these polar twins should be continuously struggling.

Starting with this extract, write about how Stevenson presents religion and morality in The Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde.

Grade 9 Model Answer

Stevenson presents religion and morality as powerful but deeply troubled forces in the novella, shaping how characters understand guilt, sin and the divided self. In this extract, Jekyll’s phrase “a profound duplicity of life” immediately evokes moral conflict. The noun “duplicity” suggests deceit and dishonesty, carrying strong ethical and even religious connotations. Yet Jekyll complicates this by insisting he was “in no sense a hypocrite”. This contradiction is revealing: Stevenson presents Jekyll as morally self-aware but also capable of self-justification. He knows his life is divided, but he tries to deny the full moral weight of that division.

The phrase “double-dealer” has a sharp, almost colloquial force, implying someone who acts treacherously in secret. By using it of himself, Jekyll implicitly adopts a moral vocabulary of judgement. However, he then claims that “both sides of me were in dead earnest”, which suggests that his better and worse selves are each sincere in their own way. Stevenson therefore moves beyond a simplistic moral framework. Rather than portraying evil as merely false or superficial, he suggests that immoral impulses can feel authentic. This is what makes them so dangerous.

The religious dimension becomes even clearer in the phrase “the curse of mankind”. “Curse” evokes biblical associations, particularly the idea of humanity’s fallen nature after the Fall. Stevenson seems to suggest that internal moral struggle is not unique to Jekyll but universal to the human condition. The image of “polar twins” continues this idea through metaphor. “Twins” implies closeness and shared origin, while “polar” stresses opposition. Human consciousness is therefore presented as a battleground where good and evil are bound together in painful tension.

Stevenson’s language here is intense and almost theological. The phrase “agonised womb of consciousness” is especially striking. The metaphor of the “womb” suggests origin, growth and creation, but the adjective “agonised” transforms it into a place of pain. This image implies that identity itself is born out of suffering and contradiction. It also gives Jekyll’s inner life a dramatic, almost spiritual dimension, showing that morality is not merely a matter of rules but a profound existential struggle.

Structurally, Jekyll’s confession comes late in the novella, which means these moral reflections reframe everything that came before. Earlier events may appear to be about crime, mystery or science, but the confession reveals the deeper issue: the battle within the soul. Stevenson uses the ending to shift the novel from detective story to moral tragedy.

In a Victorian context, religion remained a dominant force in moral thinking, yet this was also a period in which scientific and psychological ideas were challenging traditional certainties. Stevenson reflects this tension by blending religious language of sin and curse with modern ideas about divided consciousness. The novella does not reject morality; rather, it shows how difficult morality becomes when human identity itself is unstable.

Ultimately, Stevenson presents religion and morality as inseparable from the novel’s central conflict. Through imagery of curse, struggle and divided consciousness, he suggests that moral life is a painful but universal battle between opposing impulses, and that any attempt to escape this battle leads only to greater destruction.

Question 5: How does Stevenson present fear and horror?

1 O God! I screamed, and O God! again and again; 2 for there before my eyes—pale and shaken, and half fainting, and groping before him with his hands, like a man restored from death— 3 there stood Henry Jekyll!

Starting with this extract, write about how Stevenson presents fear and horror in The Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde.

Grade 9 Model Answer

Stevenson presents fear and horror as overwhelming experiences that shatter language, reason and identity. In this extract, Lanyon’s repeated exclamation “O God!” immediately conveys raw emotional extremity. The repetition suggests that ordinary language has failed him; he can only respond through instinctive, almost prayer-like cries. This is significant because Lanyon is normally rational and controlled. Stevenson therefore shows horror not simply as fear of physical danger, but as an experience so profound that it breaks down the structures of thought and speech.

The syntax of the sentence contributes powerfully to the sense of terror. The long, interrupted construction delays the final revelation, forcing the reader to experience Lanyon’s shock moment by moment. The dashes create pauses of suspense and instability, as though the sentence itself is struggling to contain what has been witnessed. This structural disruption mirrors Lanyon’s psychological collapse. Stevenson uses syntax, not just content, to make horror felt.

Jekyll’s appearance is described through a series of unsettling participles: “pale and shaken, and half fainting, and groping”. The accumulation of physical detail emphasises weakness and disorientation. The verb “groping” is especially effective because it suggests blindness, helplessness and a desperate attempt to find stability. Stevenson transforms the body into something uncanny, familiar yet terrifyingly altered. The simile “like a man restored from death” intensifies this effect by invoking resurrection imagery. This has religious undertones, but instead of offering hope or salvation, the image produces dread. Jekyll’s reappearance is unnatural, an inversion of what should be sacred.

Horror in the novella often depends on uncertainty and partial knowledge. Before the truth is fully revealed, Hyde repeatedly inspires disgust and fear that characters struggle to articulate. Enfield and Utterson cannot easily explain what is wrong with him, which makes him more terrifying. Stevenson exploits this throughout the novel: the unknown is often more frightening than the visible. When revelation finally comes, however, the horror becomes even greater because it confirms that the monstrous and the respectable are the same person.

Structurally, this moment is one of the novella’s climaxes. For much of the narrative, suspense is built through hints, letters, rumours and incomplete understanding. Lanyon’s testimony provides sudden confirmation, and Stevenson ensures that the emotional impact is immense. By allowing the truth to arrive through the voice of a horrified witness rather than through direct explanation alone, he amplifies its credibility and terror.

In a Victorian context, fear and horror in the novella reflect anxieties about scientific transgression, the instability of identity and the collapse of moral order. The possibility that a respectable doctor can transform into something monstrous threatens social, religious and psychological certainties all at once. Stevenson’s horror is therefore not limited to Gothic spectacle; it is rooted in the fear that evil is already inside the self.

Ultimately, Stevenson presents fear and horror as experiences that overwhelm the mind and expose the fragility of civilisation. Through exclamation, disrupted syntax, disturbing imagery and delayed revelation, he creates a form of horror that is both immediate and philosophical, forcing the reader to confront the terrifying instability of human nature.
