Sample Answers

Q1: Duality

Stevenson presents the dual nature of human beings as an intrinsic, inescapable and ultimately destructive force that lies at the heart of identity. In this extract, Jekyll’s reflection that he recognised the “primitive duality of man” immediately foregrounds the idea that duality is not a modern or accidental condition, but something deeply embedded within human nature. The adjective “primitive” carries connotations of something ancient, instinctive and uncontrollable, suggesting that beneath the veneer of civilisation lies a more savage and irrational self. This directly challenges Victorian ideals of rationality and moral self-discipline, exposing the fragility of social respectability.

Stevenson further develops this concept through the phrase “radically both”, where the adverb “radically” emphasises that duality is fundamental and inseparable from identity. The semantic field of division, seen in “two natures” and “separation”, reinforces the idea of internal conflict, while also foreshadowing Jekyll’s scientific attempt to physically divide these aspects. Structurally, this moment operates as anagnorisis, a moment of tragic realisation, yet it is deeply ironic as Jekyll only fully understands the consequences after they have become irreversible. This aligns the novella with elements of tragic form, where knowledge comes too late to prevent downfall.

Stevenson also employs philosophical and abstract language, elevating Jekyll’s confession beyond a personal reflection into a universal statement about humanity. The declarative tone suggests certainty, positioning Jekyll as both a scientist and a theorist of human nature. However, this authority is undermined by his failure to control Hyde, indicating that intellectual understanding does not equate to moral control.

From a contextual perspective, Stevenson is engaging with Victorian anxieties surrounding repression and hypocrisy. The strict moral codes of the time encouraged individuals to conceal their desires, leading to a division between public and private selves. Jekyll embodies this tension, as his desire to maintain respectability drives him to separate his darker impulses rather than confront them. Critics have linked this to emerging psychological theories, which began to explore the complexity of the human mind.

Ultimately, Stevenson presents duality as both universal and dangerous. By suggesting that all individuals are “truly two”, he undermines the idea of moral certainty and warns that attempts to deny or suppress this duality will inevitably result in loss of control and self-destruction.

Q2: Violence and Evil

Stevenson presents violence as visceral, shocking and deeply symbolic of humanity’s capacity for evil. In this extract, Hyde’s actions are described using the simile “ape-like fury”, which dehumanises him and aligns him with primitive, animalistic behaviour. The noun “ape” evokes Darwinian imagery, reflecting contemporary fears of degeneration and the possibility that humans could regress to a more savage state. This reinforces the idea that Hyde represents not an external monster, but a repressed aspect of humanity itself.

The verb “trampling” suggests a complete disregard for human life, implying both physical dominance and moral emptiness. Furthermore, the phrase “storm of blows” employs metaphor to convey the relentless and uncontrollable nature of Hyde’s violence. The cumulative effect of this imagery creates a sense of overwhelming force, as if Hyde’s actions are beyond rational control. Stevenson intensifies the brutality through auditory imagery in “bones were audibly shattered”, forcing the reader to confront the violence through sound as well as image. This sensory detail heightens the horror, making the scene disturbingly vivid.

Structurally, this moment represents a significant escalation in Hyde’s behaviour, marking a transition from disturbing acts to outright murder. This progression mirrors Jekyll’s increasing loss of control, suggesting that once evil is unleashed, it cannot be contained. Stevenson carefully builds tension throughout the novella, and this scene acts as a climactic release of that tension.

From a contextual standpoint, the depiction of violence reflects Victorian anxieties about the stability of social order. The fear that beneath the surface of civilisation lay chaos and brutality was heightened by scientific developments such as Darwin’s theory of evolution. Hyde embodies these fears, representing the breakdown of moral and social boundaries.

Ultimately, Stevenson uses violence not merely to shock, but to symbolise the consequences of repression. Hyde’s brutality serves as a manifestation of Jekyll’s hidden desires, suggesting that denying one’s darker impulses only leads to more extreme and destructive outcomes.

Q3: Reputation –

Stevenson presents reputation as a dominant yet deeply flawed force within Victorian society, shaping behaviour while simultaneously encouraging hypocrisy. In this extract, Utterson’s statement that he lets his “brother go to the devil in his own way” reveals a reluctance to intervene in others’ immoral actions. The euphemistic phrase “go to the devil” softens the severity of wrongdoing, suggesting a culture in which moral failings are obscured rather than confronted.

The noun “brother” implies a sense of social solidarity, yet this is undermined by Utterson’s passive acceptance of sin. Stevenson uses this contradiction to highlight the superficial nature of Victorian morality, where maintaining social harmony takes precedence over ethical responsibility. The understated tone of the dialogue further reflects the restraint expected of gentlemen, reinforcing the importance placed on composure and discretion.

Across the novel, reputation is closely linked to secrecy. Jekyll’s desire to preserve his public image leads him to conceal his experiments, ultimately resulting in his downfall. Stevenson uses structural techniques such as delayed revelation and fragmented narrative to mirror this concealment, gradually exposing the truth behind Jekyll’s respectable façade.

Contextually, Victorian society placed immense value on reputation, particularly among the upper classes. Social standing was often more important than moral integrity, leading individuals to suppress aspects of their identity that might threaten their status. Stevenson critiques this culture, suggesting that it fosters duplicity and self-deception.

Ultimately, reputation is presented as both protective and destructive. While it allows individuals to maintain social order, it also prevents them from acknowledging their true nature, contributing to the internal conflict that drives the narrative.

Q4: Secrecy and Mystery –

Stevenson presents secrecy as a central mechanism through which tension is created and themes are explored. In this extract, Utterson’s statement “I shall be Mr Seek” introduces a motif of investigation, aligning him with the role of a rational detective figure. The playful yet determined tone of this phrase contrasts with the darker implications of what he seeks to uncover, highlighting the tension between curiosity and fear.

The imagery of the door functions as a powerful symbol of concealment. Doors throughout the novella represent barriers between public and private selves, as well as between knowledge and ignorance. Stevenson repeatedly uses spatial imagery to reflect psychological states, suggesting that secrets are embedded within both individuals and society.

Structurally, the novel is characterised by delayed revelation. Information is withheld and gradually disclosed through letters and confessions, creating a fragmented narrative that mirrors the theme of secrecy. This technique not only sustains suspense but also emphasises the inevitability of truth emerging.

In a Victorian context, secrecy reflects anxieties about hidden vice beneath respectable exteriors. Stevenson suggests that such concealment is both pervasive and unsustainable, as repressed truths inevitably surface.

Ultimately, secrecy is presented as a double-edged force: it creates mystery and intrigue, but also prevents self-understanding and moral accountability, contributing to the tragic outcome of the novel.

Q5: Science and Danger

Stevenson presents science as a powerful but dangerous force when it transgresses moral and natural boundaries. In this extract, Jekyll’s admission that he is “losing hold” of his “better self” uses metaphor to convey instability and loss of control. The phrase suggests that identity is something that can be grasped or maintained, yet is now slipping beyond Jekyll’s control, emphasising the irreversible consequences of his actions.

The juxtaposition of “better” and “worse” highlights the moral dualism at the centre of the novel, while also reinforcing the failure of Jekyll’s experiment to separate these elements safely. The repetition of “slowly” creates a sense of inevitability, suggesting a gradual but unstoppable transformation. Stevenson’s use of temporal language reinforces the idea that scientific experimentation has set in motion a process that cannot be undone.

Structurally, this moment foreshadows Jekyll’s complete loss of control, as Hyde becomes increasingly dominant. Stevenson presents science as initially promising, but ultimately destructive when driven by ambition and a desire to transcend human limitations.

Contextually, this reflects Victorian anxieties about scientific advancement, particularly in relation to Darwinism and challenges to religious belief. Jekyll’s actions can be seen as an attempt to “play God”, a transgression that leads to his downfall.

Ultimately, Stevenson warns that science without ethical restraint is dangerous. While knowledge has the potential for progress, it also carries the risk of destruction when it disrupts the natural balance of human identity.
